History about GHK-Cu

GHK-Cu was first identified in human plasma in 1973 by Dr. Loren Pickart. He discovered that this naturally occurring copper-binding peptide declines with age, dropping from about 200 ng/ml at age 20 to 80 ng/ml by age 60. Research has shown that GHK-Cu has profound effects on tissue remodeling and regeneration. The peptide activates genes involved in wound healing while suppressing inflammatory genes. It has been extensively studied for dermatological applications and is widely used in cosmetic formulations. GHK-Cu works through multiple mechanisms including stimulation of collagen and glycosaminoglycan synthesis, promotion of angiogenesis, enhancement of antioxidant enzymes, and modulation of metalloproteinases.

How to use GHK-Cu

GHK-Cu can be administered via subcutaneous injection, topical application, or mesotherapy depending on research objectives. For systemic effects, subcutaneous injection is most common. For dermatological research, topical application or intradermal injection (mesotherapy) may be preferred. The peptide should be reconstituted with bacteriostatic water or sterile water for injection administration.

The blue color is from the copper ion (Cu2+) bound to the peptide. This is normal and indicates the presence of the copper complex. The intensity of blue may vary depending on concentration and copper binding.
